# CarePath Case Study #13

## Avery Brooks (Frontline Clinic Operations via Administrator Referral)

## 1. Participant Profile

- Name: Avery Brooks (alias)
- Role: Frontline clinic operations staff
- Setting: Outpatient clinic operations context where transportation reliability directly affects appointment flow and administrative workload
- Interview path: Referred through Brenda (administrator), with frontline responses provided for this interview set
- Segment relevance: Strong operations signal — confirms transportation breakdowns create repeat administrative work, duplicated costs, and preventable cancelations across departments

## 2. Context of the Conversation

- Method: Structured validation interview (10-question script)
- Interview type: Frontline perspective captured through administrator referral
- Selection rationale: Adds practical operational detail from staff who absorb day-to-day fallout when transportation fails

## 3. Problem Identified (User Voice)

Direct quotes:

- "The biggest challenge is that many patients simply don't have reliable transportation."
- "Lack understanding of how insurance / third parties work to provide"
- "They may depend on family members, public transit, or ride services that aren't always available or on time."

Core issues identified:

- Patients often lack reliable transportation options, causing frequent appointment instability
- Transportation literacy gap: patients do not understand how insurance or third-party transportation support works
- Existing dependence on family, transit, or ad hoc rides leads to missed arrivals when any link fails
- Transportation failure commonly ends in cancelation rather than recovery

## 4. Impact of the Problem

- Patient impact: Missed care opportunities when rides fail and visits are canceled
- Clinic impact: Repeated copays, lab workflows, and insurance verification may have to be repeated after transportation-related cancelations
- Staff impact: Frontline and coordinator teams repeat the same process steps across departments, increasing burnout and inefficiency
- System impact: Coordination tools underperform when staffing is thin or key coordinators are absent

## 5. Reaction to CarePath Concept

- Participant is open to follow-up and clarification
- Strong fit with CarePath's coordination thesis: the burden is not just ride availability, but repeat operational recovery after each failure
- Highest-priority improvement requested: patient education at the clinic level so people understand transportation processes before appointment day

## 6. Existing Tools and Failure Modes

- Current approach: clinic coordinators managing transportation workflows
- Failure mode #1: manual dependence on coordinator availability
- Failure mode #2: process fragility when staff are out or unavailable
- Failure mode #3: limited patient understanding of insurance and third-party transportation pathways, leading to preventable breakdowns

## 7. Key Insights Extracted

- Transportation failures are generating duplicated administrative work (copay handling, lab/insurance re-verification), not only missed visits
- Education and expectation-setting are product opportunities, not just operational training tasks
- Staff absence exposes weak process resilience, suggesting a need for shared visibility and standardized workflow handoffs
- Burden is distributed across all departments, reinforcing that transportation reliability is an enterprise workflow problem
- This case strengthens the recurring pattern: communication and process clarity are as critical as ride supply

## 8. Validation Signal Strength

- Strong (operationally specific, workflow-focused, directly aligned with recurring failure pattern)

Justification:

- Captures concrete downstream consequences (cancelations, duplicate costs, repeated verification steps)
- Identifies the education gap as a root contributor to transportation failure
- Confirms cross-department burden rather than isolated staff pain
- Includes explicit willingness for follow-up, improving interview continuity
